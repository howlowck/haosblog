**When AI agents start communicating on our behalf, the meaning behind our words quietly disappears.**

There's a critical distinction most people are overlooking: the difference between AI-assisted and AI-autonomous communication. Using AI to sharpen your draft is one thing. Having an agent read your inbox, compose replies, and send messages without you ever touching the substance is something else entirely.

Here's what changes when we cross that line:

**1. Depth becomes indistinguishable from automation.** The signals we associate with careful human thought — thoroughness, structure, precision — are exactly what LLMs produce effortlessly. Genuine ideas get dismissed on sight because they *look* generated.

**2. Human authors face an impossible tradeoff.** Compress your argument to look human-written, or risk your thorough, nuanced writing being dismissed as AI slop. Either way, the idea loses.

**3. No one is holding the substance.** Your agent reads what their agent wrote about a report another agent drafted from data another agent compiled. The chain of communication looks intact, but no human ever meaningfully engaged with it. Consider a doctor's care plan: the scribe AI generates notes, the doctor's agent drafts the plan, the patient's agent summarizes it. No human carefully verified any of it.

**4. Identity collapses out of the equation.** When agents decide what to say and how to frame it, the output no longer carries your perspective, creativity, or judgment. It carries an approximation of what a message *should* look like.

So what do we do? I think we should **invert the tool** — write the draft yourself, then use AI to compress and clarify. Push for better **disclosure** around autonomous communication. And develop a clearer instinct for the difference between **busy work and value work**: filing expense reports vs. writing feedback to someone you manage.

And as a reader? **Default to assuming the message is human-authored.** Just like we fight unconscious bias, we should fight the reflex to dismiss long-form writing. It's better to engage than to lose the insight altogether.

Some tasks are worth automating. But some thoughts are worth protecting. The hard part is knowing which is which.
